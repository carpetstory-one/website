# Meet Salim, Fourth Generation

The loom operates in a rhythm that borders on trance. The _thwack_ of the panja (the heavy iron comb used to beat down the weft) echoes through the open courtyard of the atelier. At the center of this rhythm is Salim.

Salim is a fourth-generation master weaver. His grandfather wove carpets for the royal palaces of Rajasthan, and the knowledge of tension, dye reaction, and knot precision was passed down through observation rather than written text.

## Reading the Map

While modern rug maps are often printed on pixelated grid paper, Salim still prefers the traditional _talim_—a chanted script where a master caller reads out the knot colors to the weavers sitting across the loom.

> "When we sing the pattern, the rug weaves itself."

## The Physicality of the Craft

Weaving is intensely physical. Salim sits cross-legged on a wooden plank suspended inches above the floor, adjusting his height as the rug grows upwards on the loom. His fingers move with a blur, selecting the correct hue of wool, tying the asymmetrical knot around the cotton warp, and pulling the knife down to sever the yarn in a single, fluid motion.

He repeats this action up to six thousand times a day.
