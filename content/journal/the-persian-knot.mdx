# The Persian Knot, Tied Left Over Right

In the vocabulary of rug weaving, the knot is the fundamental atom. The structural integrity, the clarity of the design, and the lifespan of the carpet all trace back to how the yarn wraps around the cotton warp.

At Carpetstory, we exclusively use the asymmetrical knot, often referred to as the Senneh or Persian knot.

## The Anatomy of the Knot

Unlike the symmetrical (Turkish) knot, where the yarn loops around two adjacent warp threads and is pulled tight in the center, the asymmetrical knot loops entirely around one warp thread and merely passes under the adjacent one.

This slight asymmetry allows the knots to be packed much tighter together.

<Aside>
  The tighter packing allows for curvilinear designs—sweeping vines and circular
  motifs that are impossible to execute cleanly with symmetrical knots.
</Aside>

## The Left-Opening Tradition

In our Jaipur atelier, the weavers tie the knot opening to the left. This creates a natural "pile direction." If you run your hand down the length of the rug in one direction, it feels smooth and the colors appear lighter. Brush it the opposite way, against the nap, and it feels rougher, the colors deeper and more saturated.

> "A machine can mimic the look of a hand-tied knot, but it cannot mimic its tension."

This tension—applied differently by every artisan—is what gives a hand-knotted rug its soul. It is a slow, rhythmic meditation that has remained unchanged for centuries.
